# Objection Handling

When prospects push back, use this. The goal isn't to "win" the objection — it's to understand the concern behind it and address that.

---

## 1. "We already use Amberjack"

**What's really going on:** Switching risk. They've invested in a relationship, a process, and possibly a multi-year contract. They're not sure what they'd be giving up.

**You say:** "Amberjack is solid for assessment delivery — they've got a good model. Where we're different is two things. First, self-service: your team configures assessment language, branding, and scoring directly, without a change request. Second, lifecycle: Amberjack covers Attract through Select. We cover Attract through Track Talent — so the assessment insight you build about a candidate actually informs how you develop them once they're hired. That's the gap no one else fills."

## 2. "We just renewed our ATS"

**What's really going on:** They think you're an ATS replacement and they've already spent their budget and political capital there.

**You say:** "We're not replacing your ATS — we integrate with it. If you're on PageUp or Workday, we sit on top. We're the assessment, selection intelligence, and development layer that your ATS doesn't do. Think of it like this: your ATS tracks where candidates are. We help you decide which ones to move forward and what to do with them after they're hired."

---

## 3. "Psychometrics don't predict performance"

**What's really going on:** They've seen bad psychometrics. They've watched assessment processes that clearly favoured certain candidates and produced mediocre hires. The scepticism is fair.

**You say:** "You're right that traditional behavioural assessments often don't — they tend to reward candidates who've had the most opportunities to practice, not the ones with the most potential. That's exactly why we don't do that. We assess five dimensions of potential: adaptability, cognitive agility, emotional intelligence, collaboration, and drive. These predict how someone will learn and grow, not how polished they are today. For graduates with limited work history, that's the right question."

`[ENRICH: Paula item #4]`

---

## 4. "This looks early-stage / it's just a demo"

**What's really going on:** Risk aversion. They don't want to bet their program on a startup that might not be around next intake. Totally reasonable.

**You say:** "You're right that we're early. What you're seeing is a working product, not a slide deck — but we're not pretending to be a 10-year-old enterprise platform. What we can show you is: the product works, the assessment model is sound, and we're building fast. The question for you is whether the vision — full lifecycle, self-service, equity-centred — is worth being an early partner. Early partners shape the product."

---

## 5. "We don't have budget for another tool"

**What's really going on:** Cost, but usually it's really about how costs are categorised. They're comparing a new line item against what they're already paying — without accounting for what they're already paying in time and workarounds.

**You say:** "How much does your team spend on manual screening today? If it takes two people a full day to shortlist 200 applicants, that's real cost. How much are you paying per-engagement for assessment changes with your current provider? We're not adding a cost — we're displacing the cost you're already carrying across spreadsheets, manual processes, and bespoke vendor fees."

---

## 6. "Our grad program is too small"

**What's really going on:** They feel self-conscious about scale, or they think enterprise tooling won't work for them. Sometimes they're right — under 10 hires a year and the economics genuinely don't work.

**You say:** If it's genuinely under 10, park it — come back when the program grows. If it's 10–30: "That's actually where the pain is sharpest. You don't have a dedicated team of five running this — it's probably one person managing the whole thing across multiple systems. That's exactly who benefits most from having it all in one place."

## 7. "How is the data secured?"

**What's really going on:** Legitimate concern. They're asking because they have to — their IT or legal team will ask them the same question, and they need to be able to answer it.

---

## 8. "Can we customise the assessment to our competency framework?"

**What's really going on:** This is a BUYING SIGNAL. They're not pushing back — they're asking if the product can work for them. They have an internal framework (often tied to performance reviews or L&D) and want to know if the assessment will speak their language.

**You say:** "Yes — and you do it yourself. Open the Assessment Config page — you change the competency labels, the question language for each track, the scoring weights. No change request. No invoice." Then show them Assessment Config in the demo.
